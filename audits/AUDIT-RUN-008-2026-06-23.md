## 2. Findings

### F07 — "ISO 19011:**2026**" does not exist; current edition is ISO 19011:**2018** — **S3 (Medium)**
- **Location:** §0 ("Auditing principles (per ISO 19011:2026…)"); Appendix → Audit-discipline family; changelog v6.
- **Evidence:** ISO's catalogue lists **ISO 19011:2018** (3rd ed., "Guidelines for auditing management systems") as current; no 2026 revision exists ([iso.org/standard/70017.html](https://www.iso.org/standard/70017.html)). The Skill states a "2026" edition as fact in its foundational role section.
- **Why it matters:** It's a fabricated edition in the normative §0 — and it's exactly the failure the Skill's own **Principle 1** forbids ("confirm the current edition… including the standards named in this very Skill"). The cited *principles* (integrity, fair presentation, due care, evidence-/risk-based) are genuinely ISO 19011's, so only the year is wrong — limited blast radius, but a credibility-class error for an audit tool.
- **Impact:** Med · **Likelihood:** High (stated as fact) · **Status:** Open · **Resolved by:** R07.

### F08 — Model-risk citations are now superseded: SR 26-2 replaced SR 11-7 *and* SR 21-8 — **S4 (Low)**
- **Location:** Module AM; Appendix → Financial-services model risk.
- **Evidence:** The Skill leads with "**SR 11-7** (Fed/OCC) and the 2026 **SR 26-2**" and separately cites "BSA/AML model risk (**SR 21-8**)." Per the Fed, **SR 26-2** (issued **April 17, 2026**) *"supersedes and replaces SR letter 11-7 … and SR letter 21-8"* ([federalreserve.gov SR 26-2](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/SR2602.htm)). So both cited letters are now superseded, not co-current. SR 26-2 also expressly puts **generative/agentic AI models out of scope** (to be addressed separately) — a routing nuance worth noting.
- **Why it matters:** The Skill should lead with SR 26-2 as current and mark 11-7/21-8 historical; otherwise it points users at withdrawn guidance.
- **Impact:** Low–Med · **Likelihood:** Med · **Status:** Open · **Resolved by:** R08.

### F09 — Module AO describes the superseded NIST SP 800-61 **r2** lifecycle while citing **r3** — **S4 (Low)**
- **Location:** Module AO (Security Ops/IR/Forensics).
- **Evidence:** Module AO frames IR as the four-phase lifecycle "preparation; detection & analysis; containment/eradication/recovery; post-incident lessons" — the **Rev 2** model — yet cites "NIST SP 800-61 … (Validate editions — 800-61 r3)." **Rev 3** (published **April 3, 2025**) deliberately **eliminates that rigid lifecycle** and remaps IR onto the **CSF 2.0** functions (Govern/Identify/Protect/Detect/Respond/Recover) with continuous improvement ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2025/04/nist-revises-sp-800-61-incident-response-recommendations-and-considerations)).
- **Why it matters:** The named method lags the cited edition. The four phases are still pedagogically common, but a Deep audit run per r3 would use the CSF-2.0 mapping.
- **Impact:** Low · **Likelihood:** Med · **Status:** Open · **Resolved by:** R09.

### F10 — A few acronyms used in-body are absent from the Glossary — **S5 (Info)**
- **Location:** Module AQ / others — **MITC**, **CFA**, **IOSCO**, **RMF** (NIST AI RMF) appear in text but aren't glossed.
- **Evidence:** Programmatic diff of in-body acronyms vs the Glossary. (MITC = Most Important Terms & Conditions, a SEBI term, is the most obscure.) Consistent with the Skill's own Pass B3 "undefined acronyms" check.
- **Impact:** Negligible · **Status:** Open · **Resolved by:** R10.
